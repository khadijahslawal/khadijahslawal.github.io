Body Language in the Digital World

Mon, Oct 29, 2018 8-minute read

Most of us had experienced this or something along the lines when we were kids. An adult visitor (most likely an aunt, uncle, or family friend) is about to leave after having a great catch up time with your family, and right before the visitor steps outside the door, he or she has this ‘aha moment’ and reaches out to their pocket or handbag before handing you some modest amount of cash.

As a kid, the first things that likely came to your head upon that occurrence, was all the sweet tooth things you could purchase, or the toys, or a surge of excitement for the next school day to roll, so that you could confidently brag to your peers about your new acquisition.

No words needed to be exchanged before you knew it was only right for you to smile and politely decline the offer. If you were lucky enough, the visitor really was giving you from the bottom of their heart, they pester you to collect it and could even go a mile further by handing the notes to your parent. If not, they happily return their weensy cash, and head out from the house, leaving you in dread and disappointment.

Although, I have some opinions on the good and bad side of raising kids to reject gifts. I am more intrigued by what I call “the silent conversation” that occurred. That look in your parent’s eyes was equivalent to a few words. It is similar to the look your friend gives you when you hand out your exam paper before them. Without them saying anything, you know that they are in dire need of help or they are silently confronting you with “when on earth did you study the concept of number 4 question? and why didn’t you tell me!”.

Body Language is a form of communication with our body parts using subtle cues. Understanding and interpreting correctly what these signals translate to is a key factor in understanding other people, and even being emotionally intelligent. The silent conversation, though may not necessarily be interpreted correctly by you if you do not take into consideration the following factors.

  • The first is being aware of the current circumstances and actions taking place. Using the child example, the child was aware that he was about to receive something.

  • The next factor is to be aware of the previous actions of the other person when a similar circumstance occurred. Most likely, the child has received a glare before from his/her parent when the same thing occurred.

  • And lastly, is the interpretation of the body expression of the person. A glare, definitely is not close to ‘i am proud of you’, it is more of ‘where is your home training?’

Despite using and putting into account these factors, there is no guarantee that you would correctly interpret the body language of another person. But I believe it is more popular for people to interpret right than wrong and it is easier to judge based on body expressions than words.

This is simply because the body and facial expressions most of the time are reflex actions, unlike verbal expressions, which take some few seconds of thinking before release. And that is why when someone tells you they are not annoyed with you, but you can clearly see the look of annoyance on their face, you are more likely to think they are actually annoyed with you.

TRANSLATING DIGITAL CONVERSATIONS

Well thus far, I have been talking about body language in physical proximity. However, the world is changing, and we find ourselves connected to people digitally than in a physical space.

Looking at the image above, I am sure you can definitely understand what the message sender is implying. Despite not being able to see a person’s facial and body expression, we easily translate what signals they are trying to send to us through digital connection be it email, phone call, text message, or social comments.

Interestingly, we have quickly transcended from looking at the facial expressions of people for reading their subtle signals to using other schemes for interpreting the language of a person we are connected to digitally. The first scheme we use is Tones. When listening to someone on the phone, we make sense of the tones of their voice. If they sound humorous, harsh, or formal, we translate the sound of their tone to be the same with the hidden symbols behind their audio message. A harsh tone, most definitely means the person on the other side of the phone is angry or something along the lines.

There is a chance, that you read the first few words (first line) in a neutral tone, but as you approached the next words, you would have made a few judgment of the tone of the sender to be a ‘worried’’ tone, and you read the rest of the sentences that way. But how did you do that?

You deciphered the semantics of the words as a collection, then made a quick hypothesis on what the situation is about, and the probable emotions being communicated in this very situation. Even if I had cherry-picked some of the main keywords (e.g. hospital, been, much, serious, answer, please)of the message my sister sent to me and presented them to you. In a nutshell, how we string text to inner emotions, is by making sense of the mood, tones, and circumstance of the text as a collection. However, should it really be called body language, considering we are not really making sense of messages based on a body expression?

Yes, it can still be called body language because moods and tones are body expressions. The main difference between the physical body language translation and digital body language translation is that in physical body expression, it is the other party we are communicating with that is making the expressions. Whereas, in electronic communications, we are the ones hypothesizing what expressions the person on the other side is making. As a consequence, we tend to misread people the majority of the time when we are communicating with them electronically, simply because our hypothesis may have been wrong.
